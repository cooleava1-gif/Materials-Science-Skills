# Published Article Writing Patterns

This file extracts reusable writing patterns from published materials science
papers in CBM, CCC, JBE, RMPD, and related journals. Use these patterns as
templates when drafting or polishing your own manuscript sections.

Each pattern includes: the move, a representative sentence, an analysis of why
it works, and a red flag to avoid.

---

## 1. Abstract Patterns

### Pattern A: Specific-gap opening

> Emulsified asphalt tack coats are widely used to improve interlayer bonding
> in asphalt pavements, but their early-age strength and moisture resistance
> remain sensitive to curing and formulation conditions.

**Why it works**: Names the specific material, its function, and the unresolved
limitation — all in one sentence. No broad infrastructure slogans.

**Red flag**: Opening with "Road construction is very important..." — too broad,
wastes the first sentence.

### Pattern B: Quantified key result

> The 15% WER group showed the highest mean pull-off bond strength (0.78 ±
> 0.06 MPa), 73% higher than the control under the tested curing condition.

**Why it works**: Gives the exact dosage, exact value, exact comparison, and
exact condition — all in one sentence.

**Red flag**: "The modified asphalt showed significantly improved performance"
— no numbers, no condition.

### Pattern C: Bounded implication

> The findings support WER modification as a promising approach for improving
> interlayer bonding, subject to field validation under traffic and moisture
> exposure.

**Why it works**: States the implication with a clear boundary ("subject to
field validation").

**Red flag**: "This material can be widely applied in road engineering" —
overclaim without field data.

## 2. Introduction Patterns

### Pattern D: Field → system → gap → entry

> Recent studies on polymer-modified emulsified asphalt have improved cohesion
> and adhesion through SBR, polyurethane, and epoxy modification [1–5].
> However, the balance between emulsion stability, epoxy curing, viscosity
> growth, and interface bonding remains insufficiently resolved [6,7].
> This study investigates the bonding performance of waterborne epoxy modified
> emulsified asphalt across a dosage range, with FTIR and microscopy evidence.

**Why it works**: Four-layer narrowing from field → system → gap → entry.
Each layer has a different citation density.

**Red flag**: "Many researchers studied A, B, C, D. These studies show good
results." — list without synthesis, no gap.

### Pattern E: Contradiction as gap

> Although epoxy modification enhances bonding strength [3,4], its influence on
> emulsion stability and workability is inconsistent across test conditions
> [6,8], creating a contradiction between performance optimization and
> construction feasibility.

**Why it works**: Frames the gap as a contradiction, not just a missing topic.
This is more compelling.

**Red flag**: "Few studies have investigated this topic" — weak gap, does not
explain why it matters.

## 3. Methods Patterns

### Pattern F: Compact procedure with standard

> Pull-off bond strength was measured according to ASTM D7234 using a portable
> adhesion tester (PosiTest AT-M). Specimens were cured at 60°C for 24 h
> before testing.

**Why it works**: Names the standard, the instrument, and the condition in two
sentences. Reproducible.

**Red flag**: "Bonding strength was tested" — no standard, no instrument, no
condition.

### Pattern G: Characterization with purpose

> FTIR spectra were recorded on a Nicolet iS50 spectrometer (4000–400 cm⁻¹,
> 64 scans, 4 cm⁻¹ resolution) to monitor epoxy curing through the 915 cm⁻¹
> oxirane peak.

**Why it works**: Gives instrument, parameters, AND the specific feature being
monitored.

**Red flag**: "FTIR was performed" — no parameters, no purpose.

## 4. Results Patterns

### Pattern H: Data-first sentence

> The bond strength increased with WER content up to 15% and then decreased at
> 20% (Fig. 2a).

**Why it works**: Leads with the trend, cites the figure. No interpretation yet.

**Red flag**: "As we can see from the figure, the bond strength..." —
unnecessary preamble.

### Pattern I: Quantify → compare → qualify

> The 15% WER group reached 0.78 ± 0.06 MPa (ASTM D7234, 7 d curing), 73%
> higher than the control (0.45 ± 0.04 MPa). The improvement was not sustained
> at 20% WER, where viscosity may have limited emulsion stability.

**Why it works**: Three moves in two sentences: quantify, compare, qualify
with boundary.

**Red flag**: "The 15% group was the best" — no numbers, no comparison, no
boundary.

### Pattern J: Mechanism evidence with attribution

> FTIR showed a decrease in the 915 cm⁻¹ epoxide peak for the 15% WER group
> (Fig. 3), consistent with ring-opening crosslinking during curing. This
> observation agrees with Kong et al. (2024), who reported similar peak
> disappearance in waterborne epoxy–asphalt systems.

**Why it works**: Observes → attributes → compares with literature. Uses
"consistent with" not "confirms."

**Red flag**: "FTIR confirmed the curing mechanism" — overclaims from one peak
disappearance.

## 5. Discussion Patterns

### Pattern K: Result → mechanism → literature → implication

> The 15% WER group showed the highest bond strength (0.78 MPa). FTIR analysis
> revealed epoxide peak disappearance, consistent with crosslinking. This is
> in agreement with Kong et al. (2024), though our system showed a broader
> curing window. From an engineering perspective, the 15% dosage balances
> bonding performance and sprayability.

**Why it works**: Four-layer widening from result → mechanism → literature →
implication. Each sentence serves a different purpose.

**Red flag**: Repeating the Results section without adding interpretation or
context.

### Pattern L: Trade-off discussion

> Increasing epoxy dosage raised viscosity, which may improve film build-up
> but can also reduce sprayability and field workability. The discussion should
> therefore connect viscosity to both bonding performance and construction
> constraints.

**Why it works**: Names the trade-off explicitly. Does not present viscosity
increase as purely positive.

**Red flag**: "Higher viscosity is better" — one-sided, ignores construction
feasibility.

### Pattern M: Limitation with evidence basis

> A limitation of this study is that all tests were conducted under laboratory
> curing conditions (60°C, 24 h), which may not represent field curing kinetics.
> The bonding claims are therefore restricted to the tested protocol.

**Why it works**: Names the specific limitation and states what it means for
the claims.

**Red flag**: No limitation paragraph — reviewers will find it anyway.

## 6. Conclusion Patterns

### Pattern N: Specific → general → boundary

> Waterborne epoxy modification at 15% content increased the bond strength of
> emulsified asphalt by 73% under the tested curing condition. This
> improvement is consistent with epoxy crosslinking and enhanced interfacial
> adhesion. Field validation under traffic, moisture, and temperature cycling
> is recommended before engineering deployment.

**Why it works**: Three sentences, three moves: specific finding → general
implication → bounded recommendation.

**Red flag**: "This material has great potential for road engineering" — no
boundary, overclaims.

## 7. Hedging Patterns from Published Papers

### Pattern O: Evidence-calibrated verbs

| Published phrasing | Evidence level |
|---|---|
| `showed an increase` | Direct measurement |
| `is consistent with` | Mechanism inference |
| `may be attributed to` | Speculative mechanism |
| `suggests that` | Moderate interpretation |
| `has potential for` | Engineering implication |
| `subject to field validation` | Boundary statement |

### Pattern P: Scope limitation phrases

| Published phrasing | Purpose |
|---|---|
| `within the tested dosage range` | Limits generalizability |
| `under the tested curing condition` | Limits condition scope |
| `compared with the control group` | Limits comparison scope |
| `to our knowledge, the first` | Qualifies novelty claim |
| `the generalizability is limited by` | Acknowledges boundary |

## 8. Citation Integration Patterns

### Pattern Q: Direct attribution

> Kong et al. (2024) reported that WER modification increased bond strength by
> 45% under dry conditions.

### Pattern R: Parenthetical synthesis

> WER modification has been shown to improve bond strength (Kong et al., 2024;
> Li et al., 2023).

### Pattern S: Comparative attribution

> Unlike the findings of Li et al. (2023), who used a different curing agent,
> our system showed a broader curing window.

### Pattern T: Method citation

> Specimens were prepared according to ASTM D7234 and cured at 60°C for 24 h.

---

## Usage Notes

1. **Start with the claim**, then choose the pattern. Do not retrofit a claim
   to a pattern.
2. **Adapt, do not copy.** These patterns are templates — replace bracketed
   terms with your specific material, condition, and evidence.
3. **Check evidence level.** The verb must match the evidence you have, not
   the evidence you wish you had.
4. **Keep the shape.** Each section has an expected information flow shape
   (see `hourglass-structure.md`). The patterns above preserve that shape.
