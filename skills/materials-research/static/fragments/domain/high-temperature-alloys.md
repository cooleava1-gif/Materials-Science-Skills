# Domain: High-Temperature Alloys

## Usage

Covers nickel-based superalloys (Inconel, Waspaloy, René), titanium alloys (α, α+β, β;
Ti-6Al-4V, Ti-6242), cobalt-based alloys, and refractory metals (Mo, W, Ta, Nb).
Applicable to turbine engines, rocket nozzles, nuclear components, and chemical processing
at temperatures exceeding 500°C.

## Core logic

1. Composition (γ/γ' partitioning, solid solution strengtheners, grain boundary elements) and processing (casting, HIP, forging) set initial microstructure.
2. γ/γ' morphology (size, volume fraction, coherency strain), carbide distribution, and grain boundary character govern deformation mechanisms.
3. Creep (dislocation climb, rafting), oxidation (protective/spallation kinetics), and thermomechanical fatigue interact to control service life.
4. Property validation requires testing at multiple temperatures and stress levels spanning the operational envelope.
5. Life prediction models (Larson-Miller, Manson-Haferd, continuum damage mechanics) must be calibrated against representative data.

## Key evidence categories

- Creep: constant-load/stress creep rupture (multiple temperatures, multiple stresses), minimum creep rate, time to 1% strain
- Oxidation: isothermal and cyclic oxidation kinetics (mass gain/loss vs. time), oxide scale identification (XRD, EDS)
- Mechanical: high-temperature tensile, LCF/HCF fatigue, TMF (thermomechanical fatigue), dwell-fatigue
- Microstructural: SEM/TEM (γ' evolution, rafting, TCP phases), EBSD (grain boundary character distribution)
- Phase stability: CALPHAD predictions vs. experimental (long-term aging at service temperature)
- Coatings: TBC adhesion, interdiffusion zone characterization, hot corrosion resistance

## Reviewer risks

- Creep data at single temperature and stress; insufficient for extrapolation to service conditions.
- Oxidation-fatigue interaction not tested; isothermal oxidation and mechanical fatigue treated as independent.
- Long-term phase stability (TCP phase precipitation after thousands of hours) inferred from short-term exposures.
- γ' coarsening kinetics not measured; microstructural evolution during service not predicted.
- Coating-substrate interdiffusion and degradation at service temperature not characterized over relevant timescales.
