# Journal: Road Materials and Pavement Design (RMPD) & IJPE — writing

## Read the shared facts first

Open `../../_shared/journal-formats/rmpd.md` for the authoritative formatting facts: article types and word limits, abstract rules, figure specs, reference style, mandatory statements, RMPD vs IJPE targeting, and common desk-rejection patterns.

The notes below are the **drafting action layer** on top of those facts.

## Audience and scope

RMPD and IJPE readers expect pavement relevance. A pure material study without road/pavement context routes to CBM. The reader wants to know what the material changes in road construction, maintenance, or service life.

- **RMPD**: material design, laboratory performance, road-material characterization, practical pavement-material development.
- **IJPE**: material behavior connected to pavement engineering — modelling, design, performance prediction, service implications.

## Drafting priorities

- **Pavement performance needs service conditions or accelerated loading.** Standard lab tests alone (without temperature, moisture, or aging conditioning) are a desk-rejection trigger. State the conditioning window explicitly.
- **Asphalt work needs rheological evidence.** Dynamic shear, BBR, DSR, or equivalent — performance grading must be backed by rheology, not just empirical indices.
- **Temperature sensitivity must be reported.** Pavement materials span service temperatures; a single-temperature result is incomplete. Report the temperature range and the sensitivity trend.
- **Connect lab performance to pavement implications.** Even a lab study should state what the material changes in road construction or maintenance; do not leave the reader to infer it.
- **Cite the pavement-relevant standard.** AASHTO, ASTM, EN, or national pavement standards — unnamed methods weaken reproducibility.

## Word and figure budget (research article)

RMPD/IJPE cap is tighter (~8,000 words including references). Plan accordingly:

| Section | Suggested budget |
|---|---|
| Introduction | ~900 |
| Methods | ~1,500 |
| Results & Discussion | ~3,000 |
| Conclusion | ~400 |
| References | ~600 (within the cap) |

Keep figures focused on rheology and conditioned performance; avoid redundant empirical-index plots when rheology already tells the story.

## Desk-rejection patterns to pre-empt

From the shared facts file: no pavement context, missing service-condition testing, and generic polymer-modifier framing. Before drafting, check:

- [ ] Is pavement relevance stated in the first paragraph?
- [ ] Are service conditions (temperature, moisture, aging) reported for every performance claim?
- [ ] Is rheological evidence supplied for asphalt work?
- [ ] Is temperature sensitivity reported, not a single-temperature point?

## Cover letter

State in three sentences: what the material changes in road construction or maintenance, the service condition tested, and why it matters to the pavement community.
