# Journal: Journal of Building Engineering (JBE) — writing

## Read the shared facts first

Open `../../_shared/journal-formats/jbe.md` for the authoritative formatting facts: article types and word limits, abstract rules, figure specs, reference style, mandatory statements, and common desk-rejection patterns.

The notes below are the **drafting action layer** on top of those facts.

## Audience and scope

JBE readers expect building-engineering application. A pure material study without building context routes elsewhere. The reader wants to know what the material contributes to building performance, durability, thermal/acoustic behavior, repair, or sustainability. Road/pavement focus routes to CBM, RMPD, or IJPE.

## Drafting priorities

- **Building-system perspective is mandatory.** Connect material data to building performance — not isolated lab optimization. State what the material changes at the building scale.
- **Energy efficiency and sustainability indicators must be quantified.** If sustainability is claimed, report the metric (embodied carbon, LCA stage, energy use intensity); unquantified sustainability reads as decoration.
- **Multi-performance trade-offs are expected.** Buildings demand simultaneous thermal, mechanical, durability, and sometimes fire performance. Show the trade-off, do not hide the property that did not improve.
- **Boundary conditions must be explicit.** Building-service conditions (temperature cycles, humidity, fire exposure, load duration) bound the result; state them.
- **Case relevance strengthens the paper.** Even a lab study should reference a building application context (envelope, structural, repair, interior).

## Word and figure budget (research article)

Typical 6,000–10,000-word band. JBE papers often span multiple performance dimensions:

| Section | Suggested budget |
|---|---|
| Introduction | ~1,000 |
| Methods | ~1,800 |
| Results & Discussion | ~3,500 |
| Conclusion | ~500 |

Plan figures around the multi-performance story; a trade-off plot (e.g., thermal conductivity vs. compressive strength) often earns its place.

## Desk-rejection patterns to pre-empt

From the shared facts file: no building context, road/pavement focus, missing building-system connection, and isolated lab optimization. Before drafting, check:

- [ ] Is the building-engineering application stated in the first paragraph?
- [ ] If sustainability is claimed, is a quantified metric reported?
- [ ] Are multi-performance trade-offs shown, not hidden?
- [ ] Are building-service boundary conditions explicit?

## Cover letter

State in three sentences: the building-engineering contribution, the quantified performance or sustainability metric, and why it matters to the building-engineering community.
