# Section: Discussion (writing)

> **Domain context**: The `domain` axis has loaded domain-specific writing guidance for [detected domain]. The mechanism evidence standards below vary by material family — the domain guide contains field-specific characterization expectations. Use both together.

Discussion interprets results; it does not repeat them figure by figure. The reader already saw the numbers — tell them what the numbers mean, how they compare to prior work, and where they stop holding.

## Default structure

`result meaning -> comparison with literature -> mechanism evidence -> rival explanations excluded -> limitation boundary`

## Drafting rules

- **Interpret, do not re-summarize.** Reference results by trend, not by re-stating the numbers.
- **Address rival explanations before generalizing.** Reviewers look for this. If an alternative mechanism could explain the trend, name it and say why it is less likely.
- **Hedging strength must match evidence strength.** Do not promote a `consistent with` finding to `demonstrates` wording. Calibrate verbs: `show` / `demonstrate` (strong) > `suggest` / `indicate` (moderate) > `may` / `could` (weak).
- **Limitations come from inside the paper, not from generic disclaimers.** Name the specific condition, sample, or dataset where the result stops holding.
- **Link each interpretation to characterization evidence.** Do not infer mechanism from performance alone; tie the mechanism claim to the spectroscopy, microscopy, or thermal evidence that supports it.

## Sentence syntax

Discussion sentences interpret:

- `may reflect`
- `suggests that`
- `could indicate`
- `is likely due to`
- `is consistent with`

## Comparison with literature

- Compare against work in the **same material system and test condition**. A fair comparison names the system, the scale, and the conditioning.
- If the literature value differs, explain why (composition, processing, test standard, scale) before claiming superiority.
- Do not cherry-pick the weakest literature value as the comparator.

## Mechanism evidence by domain

Refer to the domain writing guide for the full list. Key techniques per domain:

- **Civil**: FTIR (bonding), SEM (morphology), fluorescence microscopy (asphalt distribution), rheology (viscoelasticity), TGA/DSC (thermal stability).
- **Ceramics**: XRD (phase), SEM/TEM (microstructure), EDS (composition), density/porosity, Weibull (strength distribution).
- **Metals**: OM/SEM (microstructure), EBSD (texture), TEM (dislocation/precipitate), fractography (failure mode), hardness.
- **Polymers**: DSC (Tg/crystallinity), TGA (decomposition), DMA (viscoelasticity), FTIR (chemistry), SEM (fracture surface).
- **Functional**: XRD/Raman/XPS (structure/chemistry), TEM (nanostructure), UV-vis (optical), electrochemical impedance (transport).
- **Nanomaterials**: TEM (size/shape statistics), XRD-Scherrer (crystallite size), DLS (hydrodynamic size), UV-vis/Raman (optical/structural).

## Common failure modes

- Re-summarizing Results instead of interpreting them.
- Skipping rival explanations.
- Omitting boundaries — when does the interpretation stop holding?
- Inferring mechanism from performance without characterization evidence.
- Future-work statements that read as marketing, not as honest open questions.

## Short rule to memorize

- Results = what we observed
- Discussion = how we understand it, and when it may fail

## Domain checklist

- [ ] Is each interpretation tied to characterization evidence, not just performance?
- [ ] Are rival explanations addressed, not ignored?
- [ ] Is the limitation specific to this paper, not a generic disclaimer?
- [ ] Is the literature comparison fair (same system, scale, conditioning)?
