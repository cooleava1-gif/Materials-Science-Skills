# Section: Title (writing)

> **Domain context**: The `domain` axis has loaded domain-specific writing guidance for [detected domain]. The title patterns below apply generally; the domain guide contains field-specific terminology to surface in searchable titles.

## Default pattern

Prefer concrete titles that combine:

`system / object + action / capability + application or consequence`

## Materials-specific title patterns

Materials papers commonly use one of these patterns:

- **Material system + performance + method**: `Polyurethane-modified asphalt with improved high-temperature rutting resistance: rheology and morphology`
- **Design strategy + performance breakthrough**: `Interfacial bridging via graphene oxide for 30% higher interlaminar shear in carbon-fiber composites`
- **Mechanism + consequence**: `Pore refinement by nano-SiO2 doubles chloride resistance in blended cement`
- **Comparison + boundary**: `Hybrid fiber reinforcement versus single-fiber systems in ultra-high-performance concrete under cyclic loading`

## Drafting rules

- Tell the reader what to expect; do not be a slogan.
- Be searchable: include the material system and the key technique or property a researcher would search for.
- Be substantiated by data — if a number or quantitative word appears (`30% higher`, `doubles`), verify it against the manuscript.
- Create curiosity without sacrificing credibility. A hook is only acceptable if the claim remains fully defensible.
- Name the material system explicitly; generic titles (`A study on modified asphalt`) are weak.

## Things to avoid

- Grant-style aims (`Toward …`, `A study of …`, `Research on …`).
- Overbroad field claims (`A new era of …`, `Revolutionizing …`, `Ultimate …`).
- Question titles unless the paper genuinely answers the question.
- Jargon a non-specialist in the target audience would not recognize.
- Vague modifiers (`novel`, `advanced`, `high-performance`) without a bounded comparator in the title.

## When asked for alternatives

Generate 3-5 candidates spanning:

1. **Declarative / finding-led**: states the result directly.
2. **System + method**: emphasizes the material system and the technique.
3. **Strategy + outcome**: emphasizes the design move and the performance gain.
4. **(Rarely) question**: only if the paper genuinely answers it.

Mark the most defensible one. Verify any quantitative claim in each candidate against the manuscript data.

## Checklist

- [ ] Does the title name the material system?
- [ ] Is every quantitative word in the title verified against the manuscript?
- [ ] Is the title searchable by the field's standard terms?
- [ ] Does the title avoid grant-style and overbroad patterns?
