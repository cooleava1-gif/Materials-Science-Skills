# Language: English source (writing)

When the source notes are already in English, default to standard scientific-English drafting conventions for materials manuscripts.

## Sentence rules

- Aim for `10-30` word sentences in drafts.
- One main proposition per sentence; split if the sentence carries two.
- Prefer subject-verb-object order. Avoid stacked prepositional chains.
- Avoid em dashes as prose punctuation in drafts unless the user explicitly asks. Use commas, parentheses, or short sentences.

## Paragraph rules

- One paragraph, one message.
- The first sentence is the topic or claim.
- Each subsequent sentence has an explicit relation to the previous one: cause, comparison, restriction, example.
- Do not stack two ideas into one paragraph. If a new idea appears, start a new paragraph.

## Diction and verb calibration

- Calibrate verbs to evidence strength: `show` / `demonstrate` (strong, with statistics or mechanism) > `suggest` / `indicate` (moderate) > `may` / `could` (weak, correlation only).
- Avoid marketing verbs (`leverages`, `enables`, `empowers`, `unlocks`) unless they carry concrete information.
- Use `robust`, `novel`, `unique`, `significant` sparingly — `significant` only with statistical support; the others only when a bounded comparator is stated.
- Avoid universal claims (`always`, `never`, `comprehensive`, `all`) in materials performance reporting.

## Numbers, units, and performance reporting

- Report quantitative results with units, uncertainty, and sample size: `42.3 ± 1.1 MPa (n = 5)`, not `~42 MPa`.
- Use SI units; non-SI units (psi, °F) acceptable only when matching a cited test standard.
- Keep consistent significant figures across comparisons; do not inflate precision by adding decimals.
- Percentages need a baseline: `15% higher than the control`, not `15% improvement`.
- Temperature and atmosphere must accompany any thermal result (e.g., `cured at 20 ± 2 °C, 95% RH`).

## Abbreviations and terminology

- Define every abbreviation at first use in both abstract and body: `ordinary Portland cement (OPC)`.
- Follow the terminology ledger loaded by the `domain` axis; do not invent synonyms for established terms.
- Distinguish material, phase, and property names (e.g., `C-S-H` vs `CH` vs `calcium hydroxide`).
