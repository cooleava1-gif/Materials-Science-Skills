# Civil / Construction Materials — Writing Guide

Loaded automatically when the domain is civil/construction materials (asphalt, cement, concrete, pavement, building materials).

## Domain narrative arc

The strongest arc for civil materials follows a **service-driven logic**:

1. Engineering context: real-world infrastructure problem (e.g., moisture damage, cracking, durability loss, thermal inefficiency).
2. Material limitation: current material's performance boundary under service conditions.
3. Modification or design rationale: why this additive, binder, gradation, or process was chosen.
4. Performance evidence: controlled test data under relevant standards and conditions.
5. Mechanism evidence: microstructural, chemical, or rheological characterization linking design to performance.
6. Engineering boundary: what the lab result means for field application.

## Key evidence chain

Modification/design → fresh/early-age properties → mechanical performance → durability → mechanism

Each link must be supported by at least one characterization method. Mechanism claims require ≥2 complementary techniques.

## Section guidance

### Introduction
- Start from the **engineering pain point** (not the material's chemistry).
- Define the service environment explicitly (traffic, climate, exposure).
- Gap should be: "current solution improves X but sacrifices Y under condition Z."
- Avoid: generic "few studies exist" without naming what evidence is missing.

### Methods
- Report all **standard numbers** (ASTM, EN, JTG, GB/T) for every test.
- Include specimen preparation, curing/conditioning parameters, and number of replicates.
- For modified systems: report dosage range, mixing protocol, and storage/handling conditions.

### Results
- Lead with the **performance trend** (e.g., "bond strength increased up to 15% epoxy content, then decreased").
- Report variability (standard deviation, error bars) for every quantitative result.
- Use figures for trends, tables for exact values.

### Discussion
- Connect each performance trend back to a microstructural or chemical observation.
- Compare with literature: "similar improvements were reported by [Ref] under [condition], but the magnitude differs because [reason]."
- Acknowledge competing mechanisms (e.g., improved adhesion vs. reduced storage stability).

### Conclusion
- State what was done, what was found, and under what **conditions** the finding holds.
- Include limitations: lab-to-field gap, test duration, material variability.
- Do not extrapolate to unverified conditions.

## Reviewer-sensitive points in civil materials

- ⚠️ "Significant improvement" without statistical test or effect size.
- ⚠️ Mechanism inferred from performance alone (no FTIR, SEM, thermal evidence).
- ⚠️ Dosage optimum claimed without showing the full dosage-response curve (including the descending branch).
- ⚠️ Field applicability claimed without field validation or at least relevant accelerated aging.
- ⚠️ Standard numbers cited but test conditions deviate from the standard.

## Common pitfalls

| Pitfall | Example | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Overclaiming field readiness | "has great potential for pavement engineering" | Replace with "laboratory results suggest further field validation is warranted" |
| Ignoring competing property | focus only on strength gain, ignoring workability loss | Always report the trade-off |
| Mechanism by association | FTIR peak changes attributed to curing without kinetic data | Name what evidence supports each mechanism claim |
| Citation stacking | 5 citations at end of a sentence without specific claim linkage | Each citation should anchor a specific claim |
