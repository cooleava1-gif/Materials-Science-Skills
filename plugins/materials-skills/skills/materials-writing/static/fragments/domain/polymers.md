# Polymers — Writing Guide

Loaded automatically when the domain is polymer materials (thermoplastics, thermosets, elastomers, composites).

## Domain narrative arc

The strongest arc for polymer manuscripts follows a **processing-structure-property** logic:

1. Application context: what engineering need drives polymer selection (lightweight, flexibility, thermal/chemical resistance).
2. Material limitation: trade-off inherent to the polymer class (e.g., strength vs. toughness, processability vs. thermal stability).
3. Design strategy: why this monomer, filler, crosslinker, blend ratio, or processing method.
4. Property evidence: mechanical, thermal, rheological, or barrier performance under relevant conditions.
5. Structure evidence: DSC, TGA, DMA, FTIR, XRD, SEM/TEM linking morphology to properties.
6. Processing boundary: how the result depends on processing window (temperature, pressure, cooling rate).

## Key evidence chain

Formulation/processing → thermal properties (Tg, Tm, Tc, Td) → mechanical/rheological performance → morphology → application property

Thermal transitions (DSC) and degradation (TGA) are the minimum characterization for any new polymer formulation.

## Section guidance

### Introduction
- Emphasize the **property trade-off** that motivates the work.
- Situate the work within polymer class (thermoplastic vs. thermoset, amorphous vs. semi-crystalline).
- Gap should identify what combination of properties has not been achieved.

### Methods
- Report full processing parameters: temperature profile, pressure, time, cooling rate, post-curing conditions.
- For blends/composites: composition in phr or wt%, mixing order and duration.
- Specify testing standards and specimen conditioning (temperature, humidity, dwell time).

### Results
- Thermal characterization first (DSC/TGA) to establish the material's processing window.
- Mechanical properties: report both strength and elongation/toughness to show the trade-off.
- For composites: report fiber/matrix interface evidence, not just bulk properties.

### Discussion
- Link property changes to morphological or chemical changes.
- Compare with neat polymer or commercial grades; explain why your formulation differs.
- Address the trade-off explicitly: "the X% improvement in strength came at a Y% reduction in elongation."

### Conclusion
- Specify the polymer system, processing conditions, and property envelope.
- Note limitations: long-term creep, UV/thermal aging, recyclability concerns.

## Reviewer-sensitive points in polymers

- ⚠️ Mechanical testing without thermal characterization (no Tg/Tm known).
- ⚠️ "Improved compatibility" claimed without SEM/TEM or thermal (single Tg) evidence.
- ⚠️ Crosslinking claimed without gel content, DSC exotherm, or FTIR evidence.
- ⚠️ Degradation temperature reported without TGA curve or onset definition.
- ⚠️ Recycling or biodegradability claims without relevant testing.

## Common pitfalls

| Pitfall | Example | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Ignoring thermal history | DSC results without specifying heating/cooling rate | Report all thermal protocol parameters |
| Overclaiming compatibility | "good compatibility" based on mechanical data only | Add SEM or DMA evidence of miscibility |
| Confusing Tg with Tm | Reporting melting as glass transition | Identify transitions correctly from DSC |
| Missing processing window | recommending cure cycle without kinetic data | Report DSC isothermal or dynamic cure data |
