# Domain: Refractories

Covers fireclay, high-alumina, magnesia-carbon, magnesia-chrome, silica, and spinel-based refractory bricks and castables for steel, cement, glass, and petrochemical furnaces.

## Core logic

1. Raw material purity (SiO2/Al2O3/MgO ratio, impurity levels like Fe2O3, Na2O) sets baseline refractoriness and corrosion resistance.
2. Formulation (aggregate size distribution, binder type, additives) controls green strength and sintered microstructure.
3. Firing schedule develops bonding phases (ceramic, carbon, phosphate); under-fired products lack hot strength.
4. Hot properties under load (RUL, creep, hot MOR) are the true service indicators, not cold crushing strength alone.
5. Thermal shock resistance (Rst parameter, quench tests) must match the specific heating/cooling cycle of the application.
6. Slag/glass corrosion resistance depends on wetting, penetration, and dissolution; test must use representative slag composition.
7. Service life prediction requires combining thermo-mechanical, thermo-chemical, and thermal cycling data.

## Key evidence categories

- Refractoriness under load (RUL, ISO 1893) and creep (ISO 3187)
- Hot modulus of rupture (HMOR) at service temperature
- Thermal shock resistance (residual strength after quench cycles)
- XRD: phase assemblage in fired product (mullite, corundum, spinel, periclase)
- SEM/EDS: bond phases, slag penetration depth, corrosion interface
- Cold crushing strength and apparent porosity (baseline only)

## Reviewer risks

- Lab-scale specimens vs. industrial brick gap; scale effects on density gradient and firing uniformity not addressed.
- Thermal cycling not tested; single-thermal-shock data do not predict cyclic service behavior.
- Slag corrosion ignored or tested with non-representative slag composition.
- Creep data reported without specifying load, temperature, and atmosphere.
- Carbon-bonded refractories tested in air rather than reducing/inert atmosphere.
