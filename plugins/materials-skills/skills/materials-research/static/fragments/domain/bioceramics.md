# Domain: Bioceramics

Covers hydroxyapatite (HA), tricalcium phosphate (β-TCP), bioactive glasses (45S5, 58S), zirconia dental/orthopedic implants, and calcium phosphate cements.

## Core logic

1. Synthesis route (wet precipitation, sol-gel, solid-state, melt-quench) controls Ca/P ratio, crystallinity, and phase purity of calcium phosphates.
2. Bioactivity (apatite layer formation on SBF) must be separated from biocompatibility (cell viability) and resorbability (dissolution rate).
3. Porosity (macro/micro) promotes bone ingrowth but reduces strength; the tradeoff must be quantified, not assumed.
4. Mechanical performance (compressive strength, hardness, fracture toughness) must be tested in wet/simulated-body conditions, not just dry.
5. Degradation kinetics (mass loss, ion release, pH change) determine whether the material resorbs faster or slower than new bone forms.
6. In vitro results (MTT, ALP, cell morphology) are necessary but not sufficient for biocompatibility claims; in vivo or ISO 10993 data strengthen the evidence.

## Key evidence categories

- XRD: HA phase purity, Ca/P ratio confirmation, β-TCP/HA ratio in biphasic systems
- FTIR: functional groups (PO4³⁻, OH⁻, CO3²⁻ substitution)
- SEM: particle/grain morphology, porosity architecture, apatite layer after SBF
- MTT/cytotoxicity assays with statistical analysis and positive/negative controls
- SBF immersion: mass change, ion concentration (ICP), surface apatite
- Compressive/flexural strength (wet testing, n ≥ 6)

## Reviewer risks

- Biocompatibility overclaimed from cytotoxicity assay alone; ISO 10993 requires broader testing.
- Degradation kinetics missing; material labeled "bioresorbable" without dissolution data.
- Mechanical-biological tradeoff ignored; high porosity for bioactivity without acknowledging strength loss.
- Ca/P ratio not verified; off-stoichiometry HA behaves differently in vivo.
- SBF testing duration too short (hours vs. weeks) for meaningful apatite formation claims.
