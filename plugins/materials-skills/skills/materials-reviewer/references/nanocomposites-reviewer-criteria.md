# Polymer and Ceramic Matrix Nanocomposites Review Criteria

## Matrix and filler

Critical checkpoints:

1. Matrix polymer or ceramic must be identified by grade, supplier, molecular weight, or composition.
2. Filler must be characterized by type, size, shape, surface area, and supplier or synthesis route.
3. Surface treatment or coupling agent must be described with chemical identity and loading.
4. Filler loading must be reported as mass fraction, volume fraction, or both, with verification method.
5. Processing route must include mixing method, shear rate/time, temperature, and curing/sintering conditions.

Common weaknesses:

- Filler loading reported only as mass fraction when properties depend on volume fraction.
- Surface functionalization assumed from reagent addition without characterization.
- Dispersion quality not assessed before property claims.
- Processing conditions insufficient to reproduce the composite.

## Dispersion and interface

Critical checkpoints:

1. Filler dispersion must be evaluated by SEM, TEM, or X-ray tomography with quantitative metrics where possible.
2. Agglomerate size distribution must be reported.
3. Interfacial adhesion must be inferred from mechanical fractography or direct measurements.
4. Interphase properties must be discussed for property enhancement claims.
5. Percolation threshold must be identified for electrical or thermal conductivity claims.

Common weaknesses:

- Dispersion assessed only by optical microscopy or low-magnification SEM.
- Large agglomerates ignored in property interpretation.
- Interface improvement claimed without imaging or chemical evidence.
- Percolation claimed without conductivity-loading curve.

## Mechanical properties

Critical checkpoints:

1. Tensile, flexural, or impact tests must follow recognized standards with specimen geometry and strain rate.
2. Matrix-only controls must be tested under identical conditions.
3. Property enhancement must be reported as absolute values, not just percentage increases.
4. Fracture surfaces must be examined to identify failure mechanisms.
5. Reproducibility must be shown with statistics (n ≥ 5 preferred).

Common weaknesses:

- Only percentage improvement reported without absolute values.
- Different test conditions used for composite and neat matrix.
- Fractography absent despite toughness claims.
- Statistics omitted or based on a small number of specimens.

## Functional properties

Critical checkpoints:

1. Thermal conductivity must include measurement method, temperature, and filler orientation.
2. Electrical conductivity must report filler loading, percolation behavior, and measurement geometry.
3. Barrier properties must quantify permeability reduction and tortuosity.
4. Flame retardancy must include cone calorimetry, UL-94 rating, or limiting oxygen index.
5. Aging and environmental stability must be tested under relevant conditions.

Common weaknesses:

- Thermal conductivity compared without considering density or filler alignment.
- Electrical conductivity reported without percolation analysis.
- Flame retardancy inferred from TGA residue alone.
- Long-term stability not tested for structural composites.
