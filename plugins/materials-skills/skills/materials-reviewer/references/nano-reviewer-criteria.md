# Nanomaterials Review Criteria

## Synthesis and size control

Critical checkpoints:

1. Synthesis method (chemical precipitation, hydrothermal, sol-gel, CVD, ball milling, laser ablation) must be fully described with all parameters (temperature, time, pressure, precursor concentration, pH).
2. Size and size distribution must be measured by TEM (minimum 200 particles for histogram) and corroborated by DLS for hydrodynamic diameter.
3. Size-controlling parameters (precursor ratio, capping agent, temperature, reaction time) must be systematically varied to demonstrate control.
4. Yield, production rate, or scalability assessment must be provided when practical application is claimed.
5. Post-synthesis processing (washing, centrifugation, drying, surface functionalization) must be described.

Common weaknesses:

- Size distribution histogram missing; only representative TEM image shown.
- DLS reported without noting it measures hydrodynamic diameter, not core size.
- Only one synthesis condition tested; no demonstration of size control.
- Post-synthesis purification steps not described; residual precursors or byproducts not addressed.

## Morphology and structure

Critical checkpoints:

1. TEM/HRTEM with sufficient resolution for lattice fringe imaging; d-spacing measured and indexed to crystal planes.
2. Crystal structure confirmed by XRD with crystallite size estimated by Scherrer equation; comparison with bulk reference.
3. Particle shape and morphology described quantitatively (aspect ratio, circularity) not just qualitatively.
4. Aggregation state assessed by TEM and DLS; difference between primary particle size and aggregate size reported.
5. Defect characterization (twinning, stacking faults, amorphous regions) when relevant to properties.

Common weaknesses:

- TEM images without scale bar or with insufficient magnification.
- XRD peaks broad; crystallite size not estimated or not compared to particle size from TEM.
- Aggregation in dry state (TEM) vs. solution state (DLS) not reconciled.
- Crystal phase claimed without supporting diffraction data.

## Surface chemistry

Critical checkpoints:

1. Surface functional groups identified by FTIR with peak assignments to specific bonds.
2. Surface composition and oxidation state by XPS with survey and high-resolution scans; binding energies referenced to C 1s at 284.8 eV.
3. Surface charge measured by zeta potential at relevant pH and ionic strength.
4. Surface area by BET (N2 adsorption-desorption) with isotherm type and pore size distribution if mesoporous.
5. Surface modification or functionalization confirmed by complementary techniques (TGA for grafting density, NMR for organic ligands).

Common weaknesses:

- FTIR peaks not assigned; broad peaks attributed without justification.
- XPS survey scan only; no high-resolution scans for oxidation state analysis.
- Zeta potential measured at a single pH; not relevant to application conditions.
- BET surface area reported but pore structure not characterized for mesoporous materials.

## Properties and characterization

Critical checkpoints:

1. Property measurements must be performed on well-characterized dispersions or films with known nanoparticle concentration.
2. Concentration or loading must be quantified (wt%, mg/mL, particle/mL) by gravimetric, ICP, or UV-Vis methods.
3. Optical properties (UV-Vis, PL) must report peak position, intensity, and FWHM with measurement conditions.
4. Magnetic properties must be measured as function of field and temperature; superparamagnetic behavior requires ZFC/FC curves.
5. Catalytic or electrochemical performance must include control experiments (bare substrate, commercial catalyst).

Common weaknesses:

- Nanoparticle concentration not quantified; "as-prepared" used without dilution factor.
- Property measured only in pristine dispersion; behavior in application medium (biological fluid, concrete pore solution) not tested.
- Control experiments missing; enhancement attributed to nanoparticles without baseline comparison.
- Single-property focus; no correlation between structure, surface, and performance.

## Reproducibility and scalability

Critical checkpoints:

1. Batch-to-batch reproducibility demonstrated from at least 3 independent syntheses with property comparison.
2. Characterization data from multiple batches compared (size distribution, crystal structure, surface chemistry, functional properties).
3. Scalability assessment: synthesis described at multiple scales (mg, g) or scaling strategy discussed.
4. Storage conditions and shelf-life stability reported (aggregation, oxidation, property changes over time).
5. Standardized characterization protocols used to enable cross-study comparison.

Common weaknesses:

- Only single-batch results reported; reproducibility not demonstrated.
- Scale limited to milligrams; no discussion of scalability.
- Storage conditions not specified; stability over time not assessed.
- Characterization performed immediately after synthesis; long-term behavior unknown.
