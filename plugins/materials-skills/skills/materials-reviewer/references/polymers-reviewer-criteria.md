# Polymer Materials Review Criteria

## Synthesis and characterization

Critical checkpoints:

1. Monomer source, purity, and supplier must be reported.
2. Polymerization method (radical, condensation, ring-opening, etc.) and conditions (temperature, time, atmosphere, initiator/catalyst type and concentration) must be fully described.
3. Molecular weight (Mn, Mw) and dispersity (Đ) must be measured by GPC or SEC with specified solvent, standard, and calibration method.
4. Spectroscopic confirmation of chemical structure (FTIR, 1H-NMR, 13C-NMR) with assignment of key peaks.
5. Yield or conversion must be reported; purification steps (precipitation, dialysis, Soxhlet) must be described.

Common weaknesses:

- Molecular weight not reported or reported only from supplier data sheet.
- GPC conditions (solvent, column, flow rate, calibration standard) omitted.
- NMR or FTIR shown but key peaks not assigned to the proposed structure.
- Polymerization conditions vaguely described ("stirred at elevated temperature").

## Processing and morphology

Critical checkpoints:

1. Processing method (melt extrusion, injection molding, solution casting, electrospinning, 3D printing) must be described with full parameters (temperature, pressure, speed, die geometry).
2. Specimen dimensions and preparation (cutting, annealing, conditioning) must follow relevant ISO or ASTM standards.
3. Morphology characterization: SEM or TEM for micro/nanostructure, with sample preparation details (coating, staining).
4. Crystallinity (DSC or WAXD) must be reported when semi-crystalline behavior is claimed.
5. Processing-structure relationship: how processing conditions affect morphology must be discussed.

Common weaknesses:

- Processing parameters omitted or described as "standard conditions."
- SEM images shown without scale bar, accelerating voltage, or sample preparation details.
- Crystallinity claimed but not quantified by DSC or XRD.
- No discussion of how processing influenced final morphology.

## Mechanical and thermal performance

Critical checkpoints:

1. Tensile testing must follow ISO 527 or ASTM D638 with specimen dimensions, crosshead speed, and gauge length reported.
2. At least 5 replicates per condition with mean and standard deviation.
3. Thermal transitions: Tg (DSC or DMA), Tm (DSC) at defined heating rate under inert atmosphere.
4. Thermal stability: TGA with heating rate, atmosphere (N2 or air), and onset decomposition temperature reported.
5. Modulus, strength, elongation at break, and toughness must all be reported when mechanical performance is a key claim.

Common weaknesses:

- Only ultimate tensile strength reported; modulus and elongation omitted.
- Fewer than 3 replicates; no statistical analysis.
- DSC heating rate not specified or inconsistent with standards.
- Thermal stability claimed from a single TGA run.

## Aging and degradation

Critical checkpoints:

1. Aging conditions must be fully specified: UV exposure (wavelength, intensity, hours), thermal aging (temperature, duration), hydrolytic aging (pH, temperature, humidity).
2. Multiple aging durations to establish degradation kinetics, not just one time point.
3. Property retention (mechanical, thermal, chemical) must be quantified relative to unaged control.
4. Degradation mechanism must be supported by spectroscopic evidence (FTIR carbonyl index, GPC molecular weight change).
5. Environmental relevance: aging conditions must be justified as representative of actual service or accelerated test standard (e.g., ISO 4892 for UV).

Common weaknesses:

- Aging tested at a single extreme condition with no kinetic trend.
- "Good stability" claimed without quantitative retention data.
- Degradation mechanism speculated without spectroscopic or chromatographic evidence.
- Accelerated aging conditions not justified or related to real-world exposure.

## Structure-property relationships

Critical checkpoints:

1. Claimed structure-property links must be supported by systematic variation of at least one structural parameter (molecular weight, crosslink density, filler content, copolymer ratio).
2. Each structural variable must be independently varied; confounding variables must be controlled.
3. Property changes must be correlated with structural characterization data (e.g., Mw vs. toughness, crosslink density vs. modulus).
4. Alternative explanations for observed trends must be discussed and ruled out.
5. Quantitative or semi-quantitative models (scaling laws, mixing rules, percolation thresholds) preferred over qualitative descriptions.

Common weaknesses:

- Only one composition tested; no structure-property trend established.
- Multiple variables changed simultaneously; effects cannot be separated.
- Trends claimed from insufficient data points (2-3 levels).
- No discussion of competing mechanisms or alternative explanations.
