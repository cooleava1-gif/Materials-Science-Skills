# Reviewer Comment Patterns

> **Domain context**: The `domain` axis has loaded domain-specific reviewer criteria. The patterns below are universal; the domain guide lists domain-specific concerns to anticipate.

Use this reference to identify common reviewer comment patterns and prepare appropriate responses. Patterns are organized by category with typical wording, underlying concern, and recommended response strategy.

## 1. Novelty and Contribution Patterns

### Pattern 1.1: "Incremental contribution"

**Typical wording**:
- "The contribution is incremental."
- "The novelty is limited."
- "This work extends previous studies without significant new insight."
- "The findings are not sufficiently novel for this journal."

**Underlying concern**: The reviewer does not see a clear advance beyond existing literature.

**Response strategy**:
1. Reframe contribution as bounded evidence combination.
2. Identify what is new: material system, condition, evidence chain, or framework.
3. Revise Introduction to clarify the specific gap addressed.
4. Avoid defensive language; acknowledge existing work while clarifying your addition.

### Pattern 1.2: "Literature comparison insufficient"

**Typical wording**:
- "The comparison with previous studies is lacking."
- "More references are needed to support the novelty claim."
- "The authors should compare their results with [specific studies]."
- "The discussion of related work is incomplete."

**Underlying concern**: The manuscript does not adequately contextualize its contribution.

**Response strategy**:
1. Add comparison table with key variables.
2. Cite specific studies mentioned by the reviewer.
3. If direct comparison is impossible, compare trends and explain why.
4. Strengthen the Discussion section with literature context.

### Pattern 1.3: "Research gap not clearly identified"

**Typical wording**:
- "The research gap is not well defined."
- "The motivation for this study is unclear."
- "Why is this research needed?"
- "The Introduction does not establish a clear need."

**Underlying concern**: The manuscript does not explain why this work matters.

**Response strategy**:
1. Revise Introduction with explicit gap statement.
2. Add recent references that highlight the gap.
3. Link the gap to your specific contribution.
4. Ensure the gap is material-system specific, not generic.

## 2. Method and Evidence Patterns

### Pattern 2.1: "Method selection not justified"

**Typical wording**:
- "Why was [Method A] chosen over [Method B]?"
- "The method selection is not justified."
- "The authors should explain why [method] was used."
- "Alternative methods should be discussed."

**Underlying concern**: The reader cannot evaluate whether the right tool was used.

**Response strategy**:
1. Explain method-research question fit.
2. Cite standards or precedents for the chosen method.
3. Acknowledge what the alternative method would add.
4. Add limitation or future work if the alternative is important.

### Pattern 2.2: "Sample size too small"

**Typical wording**:
- "The sample size is insufficient."
- "More replicates are needed."
- "n=3 is too few for statistical claims."
- "Error bars cannot be calculated with this sample size."

**Underlying concern**: The results may not be reproducible or statistically valid.

**Response strategy**:
1. Report replicate count and statistical measures.
2. If n is small, acknowledge limitation explicitly.
3. Reduce claim strength from "significant" to "trend" or "preliminary."
4. Add more replicates if possible.

### Pattern 2.3: "Statistics insufficient"

**Typical wording**:
- "Statistical analysis is missing."
- "ANOVA should be performed."
- "Error bars are not shown."
- "Significance levels are not reported."

**Underlying concern**: The results lack quantitative rigor.

**Response strategy**:
1. Add the requested statistical analysis.
2. Report p-values, confidence intervals, or effect sizes.
3. Update figures with error bars and statistical annotations.
4. If the data structure doesn't support the requested test, explain why.

### Pattern 2.4: "Mechanism not supported"

**Typical wording**:
- "The mechanism is speculative."
- "Direct evidence for the mechanism is lacking."
- "The proposed mechanism is not supported by the data."
- "How do the authors explain [observation]?"

**Underlying concern**: The interpretation goes beyond what the data show.

**Response strategy**:
1. Add direct evidence (FTIR, SEM, XRD, etc.) if available.
2. Soften mechanism language from "demonstrates" to "suggests."
3. Add limitation paragraph acknowledging the mechanism is inferred.
4. Use the evidence hierarchy: direct observation > indirect evidence > inference.

## 3. Writing and Presentation Patterns

### Pattern 3.1: "English needs revision"

**Typical wording**:
- "The English needs major revision."
- "The manuscript requires language editing."
- "Grammar and clarity need improvement."
- "The writing quality is below journal standards."

**Underlying concern**: The manuscript is difficult to read or understand.

**Response strategy**:
1. Thank the reviewer for the language feedback.
2. State that the manuscript has been thoroughly revised.
3. Mention specific areas revised (technical terminology, figure captions, methods).
4. Do not claim professional editing unless it actually happened.

### Pattern 3.2: "Figures need improvement"

**Typical wording**:
- "Figure quality is insufficient."
- "Figures need higher resolution."
- "Axis labels are unclear."
- "A new figure is needed to show [content]."

**Underlying concern**: The figures do not effectively communicate the results.

**Response strategy**:
1. List specific figure improvements made.
2. If the reviewer requested a new figure, describe it and cite its location.
3. If the reviewer's suggestion was impractical, explain why and offer an alternative.
4. Ensure figure captions are updated to match revisions.

### Pattern 3.3: "Structure unclear"

**Typical wording**:
- "The manuscript organization is confusing."
- "The logical flow needs improvement."
- "Section [X] should be reorganized."
- "The Discussion mixes results and interpretation."

**Underlying concern**: The manuscript is difficult to follow.

**Response strategy**:
1. Acknowledge the organization concern.
2. Describe structural changes made.
3. If the reviewer suggested a specific reorganization, explain your approach.
4. Add transitions between sections.

## 4. Scope and Framing Patterns

### Pattern 4.1: "Beyond scope"

**Typical wording**:
- "This is beyond the scope of the current study."
- "This should be a separate paper."
- "The authors are trying to do too much."
- "Focus on [specific aspect] and remove [other aspect]."

**Underlying concern**: The manuscript tries to cover too much or includes tangential content.

**Response strategy**:
1. Acknowledge the value of the suggested work.
2. Explain the scope boundary of the current study.
3. Reduce claims to match the limited scope.
4. Add the suggested work as future work.

### Pattern 4.2: "More suitable for another journal"

**Typical wording**:
- "This work is more suitable for [other journal]."
- "The topic does not fit this journal's scope."
- "Consider submitting to a specialized journal."
- "The contribution level is below this journal's standards."

**Underlying concern**: The manuscript may not be appropriate for the target journal.

**Response strategy**:
1. Revise Introduction and Conclusion to emphasize journal-relevant aspects.
2. If the editor's decision is firm, do not argue excessively.
3. If resubmitting to the suggested journal, adapt the manuscript accordingly.
4. Focus on scope fit, not prestige.

### Pattern 4.3: "Overclaiming"

**Typical wording**:
- "The conclusions are too strong."
- "The authors overstate their findings."
- "The claims are not supported by the data."
- "More caution is needed in the interpretation."

**Underlying concern**: The manuscript makes claims that go beyond the evidence.

**Response strategy**:
1. Identify the overclaimed statements.
2. Revise to match the evidence level.
3. Add appropriate hedging: "suggests," "indicates," "is consistent with."
4. Ensure conclusions are bounded by the data presented.

## 5. Domain-Specific Patterns

### 5.1 Civil/Construction Materials

**Common patterns**:
- "Test standards not specified" (ASTM, GB/T, EN)
- "Curing conditions not reported"
- "Moisture conditioning not addressed"
- "Application rate not controlled"
- "Surface preparation not described"

**Response strategy**: Add specific standards, conditions, and procedures. Report all relevant parameters.

### 5.2 Polymers

**Common patterns**:
- "Molecular weight not reported"
- "Processing conditions not specified"
- "Thermal history not controlled"
- "Crystallinity not characterized"
- "Aging behavior not shown"

**Response strategy**: Add molecular characterization, processing details, and thermal analysis.

### 5.3 Metals

**Common patterns**:
- "Heat treatment parameters not specified"
- "Microstructure not quantified"
- "Standard test methods not followed"
- "Composition not reported"
- "Phase identification missing"

**Response strategy**: Add complete processing history, microstructural characterization, and composition analysis.

### 5.4 Ceramics

**Common patterns**:
- "Sintering conditions not specified"
- "Density not reported"
- "Weibull statistics not applied"
- "Grain size not measured"
- "Phase composition not characterized"

**Response strategy**: Add sintering profile, density measurements, and statistical analysis.

### 5.5 Nanomaterials

**Common patterns**:
- "Size distribution not reported"
- "Dispersion quality not demonstrated"
- "Surface chemistry not characterized"
- "Agglomeration not addressed"
- "Scale-up feasibility not discussed"

**Response strategy**: Add particle size analysis, dispersion characterization, and surface analysis.

## 6. Conflicting Reviewer Patterns

### Pattern 6.1: Contradictory requests

**Typical situation**:
- Reviewer 1: "Add more mechanism discussion."
- Reviewer 2: "Remove speculative interpretation."

**Response strategy**:
1. Identify the shared concern (mechanism evidence).
2. Add available evidence (FTIR, SEM, XRD).
3. Distinguish direct observations from interpretation.
4. Thank both reviewers; explain how you balanced their input.

### Pattern 6.2: Different novelty assessments

**Typical situation**:
- Reviewer 1: "The novelty is sufficient."
- Reviewer 2: "The contribution is incremental."

**Response strategy**:
1. Focus on the critical reviewer's specific concerns.
2. Strengthen the novelty framing in the Introduction.
3. Add more literature context to clarify the gap.
4. Do not dismiss the critical reviewer by citing the positive one.

## 7. Response Quality Checklist

When responding to any pattern, verify:

- [ ] The reviewer's comment is quoted or clearly paraphrased.
- [ ] The response addresses the specific concern, not a related one.
- [ ] The response includes concrete manuscript changes (section, line, figure).
- [ ] The response is evidence-bound (cites data, references, or standards).
- [ ] The tone is respectful and professional.
- [ ] The response stands alone without requiring the reviewer to re-read the manuscript.
- [ ] No promises are made that cannot be fulfilled.
- [ ] Limitations are honestly acknowledged where appropriate.
