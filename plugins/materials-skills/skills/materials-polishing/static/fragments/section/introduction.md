# Section: Introduction

The Introduction should:

- tell the reader why the work matters
- explain what gap it fills
- explain why that gap matters
- state what is already known
- state what remains unresolved
- state what question the paper asks
- indicate how the study addresses it

Do not summarize the Results section here. Do not summarize the Conclusion here.

## Materials-science-specific structure

A strong materials Introduction typically follows:

1. **Application context**: What engineering problem or fundamental question motivates this material system?
2. **Prior art**: What materials/processing routes have been tried? What are their limitations?
3. **Gap statement**: What specific limitation remains unaddressed? (e.g., "However, conventional GO dispersion methods lead to agglomeration...")
4. **Research question**: What does this paper aim to demonstrate? (e.g., "This study investigates whether surfactant-assisted dispersion can...")
5. **Approach overview**: Brief indication of methodology (e.g., "We employ optimized ultrasonication with SDS surfactant...")
6. **Key findings preview** (optional): One sentence summarizing the main result, if the journal style permits.

## Common failure modes in materials Introductions

- Opening paragraph reads as a textbook rather than a positioning move (e.g., "Concrete is widely used in construction..." without connecting to the specific research gap).
- The gap is implied but never explicitly named (e.g., discussing GO benefits without stating what limitation of prior GO-cement studies is being addressed).
- The transition from "what is known" to "what this paper does" is missing (no clear "However..." or "To address this...").
- Methods are previewed in detail; keep that for Methods (e.g., describing SEM parameters in Introduction).
- Literature review is a laundry list rather than a structured argument leading to the gap.
- Material system is not named specifically in the first paragraph (e.g., "nanomaterials" instead of "graphene oxide").

## Polishing priorities

- Ensure the gap statement is explicit and locatable (usually in paragraph 2 or 3).
- Verify that the research question aligns with the gap.
- Check that prior art citations are specific to the material system, not generic.
- Remove any Results preview that belongs in Abstract or Conclusion.
- Ensure terminology consistency: if "GO" is introduced, it should not later become "graphene oxide" or "纳米氧化石墨烯" without clear definition.
