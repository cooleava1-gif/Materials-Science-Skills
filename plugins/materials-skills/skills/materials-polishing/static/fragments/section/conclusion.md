# Section: Conclusion

Use the three-part close:

1. restate the central contribution
2. summarize the key evidence or outcome
3. state the implication with a boundary

Do not introduce new data in the conclusion. Always run an overclaim check here.

## Materials-science-specific structure

A strong materials Conclusion typically follows:

1. **Restate the research question and main finding** (1-2 sentences):
   - "This study investigated the effect of GO on compressive strength and durability of cement paste. The results demonstrate that optimized GO dispersion at 0.03 wt% increases 28-day compressive strength by 25% and reduces chloride permeability by 40%."

2. **Summarize key mechanisms or insights** (2-3 sentences):
   - "Microstructural analysis reveals that GO acts as a nucleation site for C-S-H gel, refining pore structure and improving interfacial transition zone bonding. The surfactant-assisted dispersion protocol was critical for achieving uniform GO distribution."

3. **State implications with boundaries** (1-2 sentences):
   - "These findings suggest that GO can enhance cementitious material performance when properly dispersed. However, the optimal dosage and dispersion protocol may vary with cement type, water-to-binder ratio, and curing conditions."

4. **Future work** (optional, 1 sentence):
   - "Further studies should investigate long-term durability under realistic exposure conditions and scalability of the dispersion protocol for industrial applications."

## Overclaim check

- Does each claim trace back to evidence in this paper?
  - If you claim "GO improves durability", you must have reported durability test results (permeability, carbonation, etc.).
  - If you claim "GO refines pore structure", you must have reported MIP, SEM, or other pore characterization.
- Are mechanism words (`demonstrates`, `proves`, `establishes`) backed by the right study design?
  - "Demonstrates" requires direct evidence. If you only have correlational data, use "suggests" or "is consistent with".
  - "Proves" is rarely appropriate in materials science; use sparingly.
- Is the scope of the implication narrower than or equal to the scope of the evidence?
  - If you tested only cement paste, do not claim "concrete" improvement.
  - If you tested only one cement type, do not claim generalizability to all cement systems.
  - If you tested only 28-day properties, do not claim long-term performance.

## Common failure modes in materials Conclusions

- Introducing new data or results not reported in the Results section.
- Overclaiming: "GO proves that nanomaterials can solve all durability problems" → too broad, not supported.
- Repeating Results verbatim instead of synthesizing key insights.
- Omitting limitations or boundaries of the study.
- Vague implications: "This study provides insights into nanomaterial-modified cement" → what insights?
- Forgetting to link back to the research question stated in Introduction.

## Polishing priorities

- Ensure every claim in Conclusion is directly supported by Results.
- Verify that hedging language matches evidence strength.
- Check that implications are specific, not generic.
- Ensure boundaries and limitations are explicitly stated.
- Remove any new data or results.
- Verify that Conclusion addresses the gap stated in Introduction.
