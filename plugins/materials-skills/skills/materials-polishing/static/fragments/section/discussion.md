# Section: Discussion

Discussion should answer:

- how the work fits within the broader field
- what has been added to understanding
- who should be credited for earlier work
- whether the findings support, complicate, or revise earlier results
- how the findings are interpreted
- when that interpretation may fail

Short rule:

- `Results = what we observed`
- `Discussion = how we understand it, and when it may fail`

## Sentence syntax

Discussion sentences usually interpret:

- `may reflect`
- `suggests that`
- `could indicate`
- `is likely due to`
- `may facilitate`
- `is consistent with`
- `can be attributed to`

Hedging strength should match evidence strength. Do not promote a "consistent with" finding to "demonstrates" wording.

## Materials-science-specific structure

A strong materials Discussion typically follows:

1. **Summary of key findings**: Briefly restate the main results (1-2 sentences), linking back to the research question.
2. **Mechanism interpretation**: Explain why the observed behavior occurred.
   - For strength improvements: discuss microstructural mechanisms (nucleation, filler effect, interfacial bonding, pore refinement).
   - For durability improvements: discuss transport mechanisms (permeability reduction, pore structure refinement, chemical binding).
3. **Comparison with prior work**: Compare results to similar studies. Do your findings agree, disagree, or extend prior understanding?
4. **Implications**: What do the findings mean for the material system, application, or fundamental understanding?
5. **Limitations**: When might the interpretation fail? What are the boundaries of the study (material system, testing conditions, scale)?
6. **Future work** (optional): What questions remain? What follow-up studies are needed?

## Common failure modes in materials Discussion

- Re-summarizing Results instead of interpreting them (e.g., "Compressive strength increased by 25%" without explaining why).
- Skipping rival explanations (e.g., claiming GO nucleation is the sole mechanism when filler effect or improved ITZ could also contribute).
- Omitting boundaries: when does the interpretation stop holding? (e.g., claiming GO improves all cement systems without acknowledging that dispersion issues may arise at higher dosages or in different cement types).
- Overclaiming mechanism: using "proves" or "demonstrates" when evidence is correlational (e.g., "GO proves nucleation effect" → should be "is consistent with nucleation effect").
- Confusing correlation with causation: "GO dosage correlates with strength" does not mean "GO causes strength increase" without mechanistic evidence.
- Introducing new results in Discussion (e.g., reporting new SEM observations that should have been in Results).
- Ignoring negative or unexpected results (e.g., if high GO dosage reduced strength, this must be discussed, not hidden).

## Polishing priorities

- Ensure each claim is hedged appropriately to the evidence strength.
- Verify that mechanism interpretations are supported by the characterization data in Results.
- Check that comparison with prior work cites specific studies, not generic "previous research".
- Ensure limitations are explicitly stated, not implied.
- Remove any new results that belong in the Results section.
- Verify that Discussion addresses the gap stated in Introduction.
