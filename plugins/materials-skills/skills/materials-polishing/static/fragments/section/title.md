# Section: Title

A strong title should:

- tell the reader what to expect
- avoid unnecessary technical language
- be easy to search
- be substantiated by data
- create curiosity without sacrificing credibility

Use `curiosity with credibility`, not empty cleverness. A hook is only acceptable if the claim remains fully defensible.

## Materials-science-specific title patterns

### Declarative titles (most common)
State the main finding or contribution directly.

Good examples:
- "Graphene Oxide Enhances Compressive Strength and Chloride Resistance of Cement Paste via Optimized Dispersion"
- "Surfactant-Assisted Ultrasonication Enables Uniform Graphene Oxide Dispersion in Cementitious Composites"
- "Self-Healing Concrete Using Bacteria-Immobilized Capsules: Design and Performance"

Bad examples:
- "Study on the Effect of Nanomaterials on Cement" (too vague, no specificity)
- "A Novel Approach to Improving Concrete Properties" (no indication of what or how)
- "Graphene Oxide in Cement: An Experimental Investigation" (no finding, just topic)

### Descriptive titles
State the topic without claiming a specific finding.

Good examples:
- "Dispersion Strategies for Graphene Oxide in Cementitious Materials: A Comparative Study"
- "Mechanical and Durability Properties of Nano-Modified Cement Paste"

Bad examples:
- "Cement and Nanomaterials" (too broad)
- "Experimental Study" (no topic specificity)

### Question titles (use sparingly)
Pose a research question.

Good examples:
- "Can Graphene Oxide Improve the Durability of Cement Paste? A Mechanistic Study"
- "How Does Dispersion Protocol Affect Nanomaterial Performance in Cement?"

Bad examples:
- "Is Nanotechnology the Future of Concrete?" (too broad, not researchable)

## Polishing approach

- If asked for alternatives, generate 3-5 candidates spanning declarative, descriptive, and question patterns; mark the most defensible.
- Strip jargon that the target journal's general audience would not recognize (e.g., "C-S-H" may be fine for Cement and Concrete Research, but "calcium silicate hydrate" is better for a general materials journal).
- Verify every quantitative claim in the title against the manuscript (e.g., if title says "25% strength increase", Results must report exactly that).
- Ensure the title includes:
  - The material system (e.g., "cement paste", "concrete", "mortar")
  - The modifier or treatment (e.g., "graphene oxide", "GO", "nano-SiO₂")
  - The key property or performance (e.g., "compressive strength", "chloride resistance", "self-healing")
  - Optionally, the mechanism or method (e.g., "via optimized dispersion", "using surfactant-assisted ultrasonication")

## Common failure modes in materials titles

- Too vague: "Study on X" or "Experimental Investigation of Y" without specificity.
- Overclaiming: "Revolutionary Approach to..." or "Breakthrough in..." without extraordinary evidence.
- Missing key terms: title does not include the material system or key property, making it hard to find in searches.
- Inconsistent terminology: title uses "GO" but abstract uses "graphene oxide" (should be consistent or define on first use).
- Too long: titles over 15-20 words may be truncated in databases. Aim for conciseness.
