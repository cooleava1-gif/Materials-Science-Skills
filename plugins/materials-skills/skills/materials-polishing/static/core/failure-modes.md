# Diagnose failure mode before editing

Before rewriting, identify the main problem:

- wrong paper type logic (e.g., treating a methods paper as a discovery paper)
- missing gap or poor positioning (e.g., no clear statement of what limitation is addressed)
- claim without evidence (e.g., "GO improves strength" without quantitative data)
- evidence without a clear claim (e.g., reporting XRD peaks without linking to mechanism)
- missing boundary or limitation (e.g., claiming generalizability from one material system)
- Results and Discussion mixed together (e.g., interpreting XRD patterns in Results)
- weak title or abstract signal (e.g., vague title like "Study on X" without specificity)
- inconsistent terminology, abbreviations, units, or notation across sections
  (e.g., "GO" vs "graphene oxide" vs "氧化石墨烯" used interchangeably)
- sentence-level clutter only (polishing grammar while structure is broken)

Prioritize fixes in this order:

`paper type -> section job -> paragraph logic -> claim/evidence/boundary -> sentence polish`

Do not sentence-polish a draft whose section job is wrong. Surface the structural problem first, then polish.

## Materials-science-specific failure modes

- Reporting mechanical properties without specimen dimensions, curing conditions, or test standards
- Claiming "novel" without comparing against the specific prior art in the material system
- Mixing characterization results (SEM, XRD, TGA) with interpretation in the same paragraph
- Using vague processing descriptions: "mixed thoroughly", "heated at high temperature", "added in appropriate amount"
- Omitting environmental conditions that affect material performance (humidity, temperature, loading rate)
- Abbreviation inconsistency: e.g., "C-S-H" vs "CSH" vs "calcium silicate hydrate" used without a consistent convention

Terminology consistency is a cross-cutting check that runs at every level: build the Terminology Ledger on first contact and enforce its canonical forms throughout the polish.
